# Encounter Structure

## Narrative Function

Choose one primary story job:

- obstacle on the way to a larger goal
- ambush or consequence of earlier failure
- introduction to a faction or threat
- climax of a character or arc conflict
- defense, escape, delay, rescue, or pursuit scene

## Adversary Motives

Define:

- what the enemies want besides simple survival
- what success looks like for them
- what makes them retreat, surrender, or escalate

This should shape their actions during the battle.

## Role Pressure Guide

- **Standard:** backbone, space-holder, guard line
- **Bruiser:** durable close-range threat, melee occupation
- **Leader:** ally engine, command pressure, rally or summon
- **Support:** buffs, conditions, control, tactical priority target
- **Ranged:** positional pressure, punishes exposure
- **Skulk:** backline threat, ambush, disruption
- **Horde:** scale and attrition with declining output
- **Minion:** cinematic numbers and fast resolution
- **Solo:** centerpiece with extra action economy or strong scene support
- **Social:** coercion, status, negotiation, emotional leverage

## Environment Pressure

Prefer environments that:

- divide the party
- reward movement or punish immobility
- create urgency
- reinforce the enemy motive
- expose hidden truths or scene history

## Escalation

Add escalation when the scene should change over time:

- countdowns
- reinforcements
- changing terrain
- collapsing objectives
- reveal of a hidden motive

## Victory States

Always define more than "defeat all enemies" when the fiction supports it.

Useful alternatives:

- hold out long enough
- seize or protect an object
- rescue captives
- escape before a countdown triggers
- break morale or force retreat
- expose the real cause of the battle
