# Tier Scaling

Use these improvised benchmarks:

- **Tier 1:** ATK `+1`, damage `1d6+2` to `1d12+4`, Difficulty `11`, thresholds `7/12`
- **Tier 2:** ATK `+2`, damage `2d6+3` to `2d12+4`, Difficulty `14`, thresholds `10/20`
- **Tier 3:** ATK `+3`, damage `3d8+3` to `3d12+5`, Difficulty `17`, thresholds `20/32`
- **Tier 4:** ATK `+4`, damage `4d8+10` to `4d12+15`, Difficulty `20`, thresholds `25/45`

## Adjustment Notes

- Moving from low tier to high tier often needs 1 to 3 extra HP or Stress.
- Features with custom damage may need separate scaling.
- Add a feature when scaling up if raw numbers alone do not change the threat meaningfully.
- Remove or soften a feature when scaling down if it dominates the scene.

## Scaling Procedure

1. Keep the role if possible.
2. Replace attack modifier, damage, Difficulty, and thresholds.
3. Review HP and Stress for how long the enemy should last.
4. Re-evaluate any feature that deals damage outside the standard attack.
5. Re-evaluate any feature that hard-controls PCs, summons allies, or bypasses normal defenses.

## Role-Sensitive Scaling

- **Bruiser:** preserve impact first, then accuracy.
- **Leader:** preserve ally interaction first, then raw offense.
- **Support:** preserve condition or support identity first, then damage.
- **Standard:** keep the stat line simple and near the middle.
- **Solo:** verify that the enemy still has enough action presence for the whole party.

## Special Cases

- **Horde:** high opening damage, then reduce sharply after enough damage is marked.
- **Minion:** fixed damage, 1 HP, 1 Stress, and a `Minion (X)` value that fits tier fragility.
- **Solo:** high damage, high durability, and action-economy support.

## Cautions

- A feature that was merely annoying at Tier 4 can be overwhelming at Tier 1.
- A feature that depends on ally density may stop mattering if the encounter composition changes.
- Scaling across multiple tiers is often closer to rebuilding than tuning.
